Before You Begin

A quick refresher on the frameworks you'll apply in this simulation.

🔬

Rapid Prototyping

Building to learn, not building to ship

Rapid prototyping is the practice of building quick, low-cost versions of a product to test assumptions before investing in full development. The goal isn't a polished product — it's validated learning. The faster you prototype, the faster you find out what works.

How to apply it

Ask: What's the riskiest assumption in this idea? What's the cheapest way to test it? Can I learn what I need from a mockup, a landing page, or a manual process before writing production code?

Example

While building the game Glitch, Tiny Speck's team built an internal chat tool to coordinate. That rough prototype — never intended as a product — became Slack. The best prototypes often emerge from solving your own problems first.

MVP Strategy

The minimum viable version that tests your riskiest assumption

An MVP isn't a stripped-down product — it's the smallest experiment that answers your biggest question. The key is identifying which assumption, if wrong, would kill the product. Then build just enough to test that assumption.

How to apply it

List your assumptions from riskiest to safest. Design your MVP to test the top one. Set a success metric before you build. If the MVP hits it, invest more. If not, pivot or kill.

Example

Before public launch, Slack tested with just 8 companies in a closed beta. The riskiest assumption wasn't "can we build chat?" — it was "will teams actually switch from email?" The beta tested exactly that.

Pivot Decision-Making

Recognising when the data says change course

A pivot isn't failure — it's a strategic redirect based on what you've learned. The hardest part isn't deciding to pivot, it's recognising when you should. Sunk cost bias, team attachment, and investor expectations all pull you toward persisting when the data says stop.

How to apply it

Set kill criteria before you start. Ask: What would have to be true for this to work? If those conditions aren't met after a fair test, pivot. Don't confuse passion for product-market fit.

Example

Stewart Butterfield shut down Glitch — a game with devoted fans and years of development — because the growth curve was flat. He redirected the entire company to an internal tool nobody asked for. That pivot created a $27B company.

🏗️

Build vs Buy vs Partner

Speed vs control vs cost tradeoffs

Every feature decision has three paths: build it yourself (full control, slow), buy/acquire (fast, expensive), or partner/integrate (fast, less control). The right choice depends on whether the capability is core to your product or peripheral.

How to apply it

Ask: Is this core to our value proposition? If yes, build. Is speed more important than control? If yes, partner. Is the capability mature and available? If yes, buy or integrate. Never build what you can borrow when speed matters.

Example

Slack chose to build an integrations platform rather than building every tool (file sharing, video, project management) themselves. By partnering with 2,400+ apps, they became the hub without building the spokes — a masterclass in strategic partnering.
